CS degree — what you actually study
These are the real subjects inside a Computer Science degree, explained simply
Programming — writing code in Python, C++, Java. This is the core skill. Everything else builds on it.
Algorithms — learning the smartest ways to solve problems with code. Like learning chess moves instead of just moving pieces randomly.
Data structures — how to organise information so computers can use it fast. Lists, trees, graphs.
Operating systems — how computers manage memory, processes and hardware under the hood. Essential for robotics.
Networks — how computers talk to each other. How the internet works. Critical for understanding robot communication.
AI and machine learning — teaching computers to learn from data. This is what makes robots intelligent, not just mechanical.
Databases — storing and retrieving information efficiently. Robots need this to remember things.
Robotics Master's — TU Munich
What you study on top of your CS base
Kinematics — the mathematics of how robot arms and legs move. How to make a robot reach a target position precisely.
Control systems — keeping a robot stable and accurate. How a drone stays level in wind.
Computer vision — giving robots eyes. How a robot recognises a cup and knows where to grab it.
ROS (Robot Operating System) — the universal software platform all real robots run on. Like Windows but for robots.
Embedded systems — writing code that runs directly on hardware chips inside a robot. Very low-level, very powerful.
Path planning — how a robot figures out the best route to move from A to B without hitting anything.
Cybersecurity certifications — the side track
One per year, studied in evenings — not full-time
Before Year 1 — ISC2 CC (free, 2 months) — basic security concepts. Do this now.
Year 1 — CompTIA Security+ ($390, 3-4 months) — your security foundation. Industry standard. Every employer recognises it.
Year 2 — eJPT ($200, 2-3 months) — real hands-on hacking practice. You actually hack into machines to pass.
Year 3 — CEH ($950, 4-6 months) — ethical hacking. Teaches you how attackers think. Directly applicable to finding weaknesses in robot systems.
Master's Year 1 — GICSP ($979, 4-6 months) — security for industrial machines and robots specifically. The most unique cert for your path.
Master's Year 2 — OSCP ($1,499, 6-12 months) — the hardest and most respected hacking cert. Optional but elite. Only do this if you have capacity.

Pick one to obsess over during your studies — not to start now, but to understand deeply
Secure robotics infrastructure — nobody is building the cybersecurity layer for factory and warehouse robots. This is your unique angle combining both skills.
Humanoid robots for developing markets — Figure and Tesla are targeting US/Europe. Africa, South Asia, MENA are completely uncovered. Cheaper hardware, same AI.

Daily structure — university years
A day that fits everything: study, faith, training, and building
5:30 AM
Wake up. Fajr prayer. 10 minutes of silence — no phone. This is your most important habit. It sets the tone for everything that follows.
6:00 AM
Deep work block 1 — 90 minutes. Study or build something. No distractions. This is your best brain time — use it before the world wakes up.
7:30 AM
Breakfast. Get ready. Commute or walk to university.
9:00 AM
University lectures and labs. Be present. Ask questions. Sit with the best students.
1:00 PM
Dhuhr prayer + lunch. Real food, not junk. 20 minute walk if possible. This resets your brain for the afternoon.
2:00 PM
University or deep work block 2 — coursework, projects, or cybersecurity cert study.
5:00 PM
Asr prayer. Short break — stretch, breathe, step outside for 10 minutes.
5:30 PM
Training — 3 days a week (boxing, wrestling or weights, 1h15m). On non-training days: build a robot project, work on certs, or rest intentionally.
7:30 PM
Maghrib prayer. Dinner with no screens. This is recovery time.
8:30 PM
Light work or reading — review notes, watch a technical video, read one chapter. No new hard problems at this hour.
10:00 PM
Isha prayer. Wind down. Phone away. Sleep by 10:30–11:00 PM maximum. Sleep is not optional — it is when your brain consolidates everything you learned.
You do not need 12 hours of studying a day. You need 4–5 hours of completely focused work done consistently every day for 12 years. That beats 12 hours of distracted half-effort every time.

The mindset that makes this work
Not motivation — these are operating principles
Faith is your anchor, not a side thing. Your prayer is not interrupting your plan — it is structuring it. Every serious builder needs something bigger than ambition to keep going when it gets hard. You already have that.
Consistency beats intensity. Two hours every day for 12 years is worth more than pulling all-nighters in panic. The engineers who built the best things were not the ones who worked hardest for one week — they were the ones who showed up every single day.
Training is not a distraction — it is the engine. Boxing and wrestling teach you the same thing that building companies requires: how to stay calm under pressure, how to take hits and keep going, and how to be disciplined when you don't feel like it.
Build while you study. The certificate or degree is proof you studied. The GitHub and the robots you built are proof you actually understand. Companies hire people who build. Investors fund people who build.
Your background is an advantage. Coming from Bosnia, studying in a French school, speaking multiple languages, understanding different cultures — these things matter enormously when you eventually build products for global markets.
Do not compare your year 1 to someone else's year 7. Most people who seem far ahead of you started earlier or had more resources. Your job is to be better than yourself last month — nothing else.
The goal is not to be rich — it is to build something real. The money follows people who solve real problems for real people. Focus on the problem, stay obsessed with it for years, and the wealth becomes a side effect of being excellent.

How CS and robotics connect — explained simply
Think of it like building a car
CS is the engine
You learn how to write code that thinks, learns, and makes decisions.
You learn how computers handle memory, speed, and communication.
You learn AI — how machines learn from experience.
Robotics is the full car
You take the CS engine and put it inside a physical machine.
You learn how to make motors move, sensors see, and arms grab.
The robot is nothing without the CS brain you already built.
Every single skill from your CS degree is used directly in robotics. Algorithms = how a robot plans its path. Networks = how robots communicate with each other. AI = how robots learn to do tasks. Operating systems = what runs inside the robot's computer chip. You are not starting over when you switch to robotics — you are adding a body to the brain you already built.
How the transition works — step by step
From CS Bachelor's to Robotics Master's
Step 1
Finish CS degree with strong grades
You need roughly 70–75% average or above. Focus on AI, networks, and systems courses — these are directly relevant to the robotics admission interview.
Step 2
Build robot projects during your CS years
TU Munich's robotics admission looks at your motivation and practical experience, not just your grades. Your GitHub showing real robot builds is the most powerful thing you can submit.
Step 3
Apply in your final year of CS
Applications typically open October–December. You submit your grades, a 2-page personal statement, a 1,000-word essay, and your CV. If shortlisted, you attend an admission interview.
Step 4
Arrive at TU Munich already ahead
Because you already know how to code in C++ and Python, and you have already used ROS and Arduino, the first semester will feel manageable. Most students from pure mechanical backgrounds struggle with the coding side. You won't.
The CS degree is not a detour. It is the reason you will be better than most robotics Master's students at TU Munich when you arrive. You show up already able to code. That is not a small thing — it is a massive advantage.
