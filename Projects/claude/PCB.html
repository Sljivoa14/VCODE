Voltage, Current & Resistance

💡 The Water Analogy

Electricity is easiest to understand through water. Imagine a pipe with water flowing through it:

VOLTAGE (V)

Water pressure in the pipe. It's the "push" that drives current. Measured in Volts (V).

CURRENT (I)

The flow rate of water. How much charge moves per second. Measured in Amperes (A).

RESISTANCE (R)

The pipe width — narrow pipe = high resistance. Opposes current flow. Measured in Ohms (Ω).

POWER (P)

Energy used per second — voltage × current. Like how fast + forceful the water is. Measured in Watts (W).

📐 Ohm's Law

The single most important formula in electronics. If you know any two values, you can calculate the third.

V = I × R
I = V ÷ R
R = V ÷ I
P = V × I

Memory trick: Cover the letter you want to find in the triangle V/IR — the remaining letters show the formula.

🔄 Series vs. Parallel Circuits

Series

Components connected end-to-end in a single loop. Current is the same everywhere. Voltages add up. Total resistance = R1 + R2 + ...

R_total = R1 + R2

Parallel

Components share the same two endpoints. Voltage is the same across each. Currents add up. Total resistance is less than the smallest.

1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2

How Components Work

🔧 Passive Components

These components don't amplify or switch — they store, limit, or block energy.

⬛
Resistor
Limits current flow. Color bands encode resistance value. Used for voltage dividers, LED current limiting, pull-up/down.
Ω — Ohms
⊣⊢
Capacitor
Stores charge like a tiny rechargeable battery. Blocks DC, passes AC. Used for filtering, timing, decoupling noise.
F — Farads
〰️
Inductor
Stores energy in a magnetic field. Resists changes in current. Used in filters, power supplies, RF circuits.
H — Henrys
🌀
Transformer
Steps voltage up or down using magnetic induction. Works with AC only. Found in chargers and power adapters.
Turns ratio
💡 Semiconductors

Materials that can conduct or insulate depending on conditions. The foundation of all modern electronics.

→|
Diode
One-way current valve. Current flows only from Anode (+) to Cathode (−). Used for rectification, protection, signal clamping.
0.7V forward drop
💡
LED
Light-Emitting Diode — emits light when current flows. Needs a series resistor to limit current. Typical: 20mA, 2–3V drop.
R = (V-Vf) / I
⤳
Transistor (BJT)
Acts as amplifier or electronic switch. A small base current controls a larger collector current. NPN & PNP types.
β = Ic / Ib
⬡
MOSFET
Voltage-controlled switch. Gate voltage controls current from Drain to Source. Extremely efficient, used in power circuits & CPUs.
Vgs threshold
📦
IC (Chip)
Integrated Circuit — thousands to billions of transistors on one chip. Op-amps, microcontrollers, timers, logic gates.
Many pins / functions
⚡
Zener Diode
Like a diode but deliberately allows reverse current at a specific "breakdown" voltage. Used for voltage regulation.
Vz — Zener voltage

🔍 How to Read a Schematic — Step by Step
1
Find the power rails first.

Identify VCC/VDD (positive supply) at the top and GND at the bottom. These are the "rivers" everything connects to.

2
Trace the main signal path.

Follow the circuit from input to output — left to right is conventional. Follow the lines connecting components.

3
Look for dots at intersections.

A dot means two wires connect. No dot means they cross but don't connect. This is critical!

4
Read component labels.

R1 = first resistor, C1 = first capacitor, U1 = IC, Q1 = transistor. Values are labeled beside each component.

5
Identify blocks/sub-circuits.

Complex schematics are made of familiar sub-circuits: voltage dividers, filters, amplifiers. Learn these building blocks.

Power Requirements & Signal Flow

🔋 Common Voltage Standards

Different systems operate at different voltages. Mixing them up can destroy components.

1.8V — Modern MCUs 3.3V — Raspberry Pi, ESP32 5V — Arduino, USB, Classic TTL 9V — Common batteries 12V — Car, LED strips 24V — Industrial 120/240V AC — Mains power
⚠️ Logic level warning: A 5V Arduino sending signals to a 3.3V device (like ESP32) will damage it over time. Always use a logic level shifter or voltage divider when crossing voltage domains.

〰️ AC vs DC

DC (Direct Current) — flows in one direction. Batteries, USB, most electronics run on DC.

AC (Alternating Current) — reverses direction periodically. Mains power (50/60Hz). More efficient to transmit over distance.

AC → Rectify → Filter → DC
📦 Decoupling Capacitors

Every IC needs a small capacitor (typically 100nF) between its power pin and GND, placed as close as possible.

This "decoupling cap" absorbs sudden current spikes and prevents voltage fluctuations from corrupting digital logic — often called bypass capacitors.

🛡️ Protection Essentials

Fuses & Polyfuses

Protect against overcurrent. A fuse blows (opens circuit) when current exceeds its rated value. Always include in power lines.

Flyback Diodes

Motors and relays are inductors — they generate a reverse voltage spike when switched off. A diode placed in reverse across them absorbs this spike.

ESD Protection

Static electricity can destroy ICs. Use ESD-safe handling, ground wristbands, and TVS diodes on external connectors.

Current Limiting

Always limit current to LEDs, sensors, and GPIO pins. Exceeding max current ratings permanently damages semiconductors.
